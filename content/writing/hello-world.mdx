# Building a Personal Operating System on the Web

I've always been fascinated by the idea of treating a personal website as more than just a static page. Instead of the typical portfolio layout, I wanted something that felt like a **personal operating system** — a place where I could organize my thoughts, writing, projects, and bookmarks in a way that felt natural and fast.

## Why Not a Traditional Portfolio?

Most portfolio sites follow the same pattern: hero section, about me, projects grid, contact form. They're fine for a first impression, but they don't encourage regular content creation or exploration.

What I wanted was:

- **Fast navigation** between different types of content
- **Easy content creation** without touching code for every update
- **A system that grows** with me over time

## The Technical Stack

Here's what I landed on:

```typescript
const stack = {
  framework: "Next.js 14 (App Router)",
  language: "TypeScript",
  styling: "Tailwind CSS",
  content: "MDX + Notion API",
  deployment: "Vercel",
};
```

### MDX for Long-Form Writing

Blog posts like this one are written in MDX — Markdown with JSX support. This gives me the best of both worlds: the simplicity of Markdown for writing, with the ability to embed interactive components when needed.

### Notion as a CMS

For shorter content like notes, project entries, and bookmarks, I use Notion as a headless CMS. The Notion API makes it easy to query databases and render content on the site. This means I can add a new note or bookmark from my phone and it appears on the site automatically.

## The Layout System

The most important design decision was the **list-detail pattern**. Inspired by apps like Apple Mail and Slack, the layout uses:

1. A persistent sidebar for navigation
2. A scrollable list of items
3. A detail view for the selected item

This creates an app-like feel that encourages exploration rather than linear reading.

## Key Lessons

1. **Treat your site like a product.** Think about user flows, not just pages.
2. **Make content creation frictionless.** The easier it is to publish, the more you'll write.
3. **Start minimal and iterate.** You don't need every feature on day one.
